Introduction

Does the university a student goes to affect their starting salary? Helping students evaluate whether university is worth it is ever more important in the context of increasing university tuition costs college-tuition and fewer well-paying jobs requiring college degrees. If a student can look at a few characteristics of an institution, knowing their association with median starting salary, it may influence their decision of whether to attend. I focus on collecting data at the institutional-level and not the individual-level owing to higher quality, government-collected data for the former and privacy concerns in the latter. What, then, are some possible interesting attributes of institutions to examine?

Perhaps most obviously, the type of the institution and whether they grant certificate degrees, graduate degrees, or somewhere in between, seems to intuitively be associated with how well their graduates do: it would seem that higher levels of degrees are more valued than lower levels in the job market. Additionally, the percentage of students graduating with degrees in lucrative fields like engineering and computer science would be the place to start, as the wealth of high-paying jobs offered in the booming technology industry would seem to increase an institution's median starting salary. It’s also interesting to look racial data, and see how some might be able to use formal methods to prop up stereotypic narratives about race, college, and wealth.

The Data

The dataset I’m using for this cross-sectional study is from the U.S. Department of Education’s College Scorecard website and contains “aggregate data for each institution, [including] information on institutional characteristics, enrollment, student aid, osts, and student outcomes” for the 2018-2019 academic year (which I used in lieu of the most recent academic year as it had fewer null and privacy suppressed measurements). Some of the 2989 data points. included for each of the 6807 colleges/universities are various statistics on completion rate, faculty salaries, student income, student demographics, student outcomes, and eventual income.

The main response variable for this study is the median earnings of students working and not enrolled 6 years after entry, MD_EARN_WNE_P6 which I use as a proxy for median entry-level salary after graduation from a school. I cleaned our data by filtering out institutions that only provided distance learning (i.e., those with the DISTANCEONLY indicator equal to 1), removing all columns corresponding to predictors not listed below, and filtering out colleges with null or privacy suppressed values for any of the predictors I are interested in. The distribution of the response variable is heavily right-skewed, which makes sense as it is similar in shape to the overall income distribution in the United States, and the average American college graduate is likely not making above the median wage in the United States right out of college even if attending college does tend to be associated with higher salaries. Log-transforming this variable made its distribution almost perfectly symmetric. I also qualitatively examined what types of schools have high median earnings and low median earnings. I saw that nursing/medical schools and engineering schools make up many of the schools at the very top of the spectrum, while cosmetology schools make up the bulk of the schools at the very bottom of the spectrum.

I noted that since the dataset contains many non-traditional institutions of higher education (e.g. cosmetology schools), only around a quarter of the schools in the dataset report both SAT scores as well as admission rates. I labeled schools that report both of these metrics as traditional universities and those that fail to report at least one of these metrics as non-traditional universities. While this approach of separating universities is prone to some amount of error (e.g. some traditional universities do not require SAT scores for admission), I believe that the number of schools that fall into this gray area is sufficiently low for the sake of our analysis. Plotting a histogram of MD_EARN_WNE_P6 for traditional and non-traditional universities I saw that the distribution for traditional universities seems to have a higher minimum, mean, median and max and is more right-skewed than that for non-traditional universities (although both distributions do display a right skew).

I will now formally test whether the distribution of median starting salaries is significantly different for the 1271 traditional universities versus the 3683 non-traditional universities. In particular, I conduct a rank-sum test to compare the distributions of median starting salaries in each university category. The only assumption of this non-parametric test is independence of observations, which seems loosely satisfied, as 6 it is reasonable that each university is independent from others. Our null hypothesis is that the true average quantile of median starting salaries in the two categories of university are the same, and our alternative hypothesis is that there is an association between category (traditional versus non-traditional) and the average quantile of outcomes, i.e., median starting salaries, of universities. I achieved a test statistic of W = 4128874 and a 95% confidence interval of [13493, 14565] for the difference in median starting salary between traditional universities and non-traditional universities, showcasing the advantage for students of attending a traditional university. This gives a p-value of < 2.2 · 10^−16 ≈ 0 < 0.05, meaning I reject the null at the α = 0.05 significance level and conclude the alternative hypothesis is true, or loosely that the distribution of median starting salaries is significantly different for traditional universities compared to non-traditional universities. Therefore, combining these data points into one large dataset might weaken the prediction and inference I can perform. Hence, for the sake of this paper not having to write every model/question twice, I focus on doing prediction and performing inference on the various predictors above, including average SAT and admissions rate, for traditional universities only.

Since I was unsure of what factors I’d take into consideration in my final model during my data cleaning, I spent a lot of time formatting a lot of factors that I didn’t end up using in my study. Results

In this section I chose to investigate specific questions regarding the association between the percentage of Asian undergraduates and median starting salary, and between the percentage of degrees awarded in each major and starting salary. You could repeat this analysis with any race, but doing it with many different races would far exceed the page limit of this paper, so I chose Asian.

Looking at the data, it’s clear that in the year 2015 the median annual earnings of Asian men and women surpassed that of all other races of men and women respectively. This correlation between race and earnings leads to the question: is there a significant relationship between the percentage of Asian students in a school and the median starting salary of that school’s graduates? To answer this question I fitted a linear model for the log of MD_EARN_WNE_P6, lm.asian, with the only predictor being UGDS_ASIAN (multiplied by a factor of 100 to change proportions to percents) with the resulting coefficients and their p-values from the relevant t-tests presented in the table below.

I found that the regression coefficients for both the intercept and the UGDS_ASIAN predictor are extremely significant with p-values that are nearly 0. From the intercept we see that in a school with no Asian students (schools which we know exist from the table), the log median starting salary after graduation is e^10.55 ≃ 38, 177. For every 1% increase in the Asian population of a school, there is an increase of 0.021 in the log median starting salary of graduates from the school, or a multiplicative increase of e^0.021 ≃ 1.021 in starting salary - a pretty drastic increase!

Conclusion

From our model, we can conclude that, in traditional universities, there is a positive modelable correlation between being asian and median salary in six years. Assessing our results, however, it is important for me to state that I do not believe that this relationship is causal. One potential confounder in the analysis above is that the distribution of majors chosen by students of different races is not uniform. Furthermore, we know that salaries are not uniform across professions. Analyzing the dataset reveals that the highest tiers of earnings is almost entirely divided between Computer Science and Mathematics, Health, and Architecture and Engineering majors. Given that Asians are over-represented in STEM fields (under which two out of three of these majors would fall), there is a strong, probable case for major and median salary to have some sort of causal relationship . Failing to acknowledge these variables leaves us open to the danger of using formal methods to prop up racist stereotypes or mindsets. Future studies should test the presence of STEM in the data, and see if the presence of major choices overshadows the impact on median salary over race.
